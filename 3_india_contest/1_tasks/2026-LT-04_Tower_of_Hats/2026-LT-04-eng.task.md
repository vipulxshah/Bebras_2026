## Body

A hat shop owner likes to create attractive displays by arranging hats into towers. However, he does not want to stack the hats randomly. Instead, he asked an engineer to design an elegant arrangement scheme:

![tower]

Each arrow shows which hat to pick up and place directly on top of the hat already there. For example, the scheme
![explain1] shows that the yellow (right) hat is stacked on the left one: ![explain2]

## Question/Challenge - for the brochures

The shop owner intended to follow the scheme and built four towers of hats. However, he made a mistake in one of the towers. Which one of the towers does not follow the scheme?

## Answer Explanation

The correct answer is (D).

The scheme describes how hats must be arranged in a tower.

To solve the task, we need to carefully compare each tower with the scheme.

Starting from the bottom of the tower, we check whether the order and types of hats follow the rule shown in the scheme. Each level of the tower must match the corresponding rule.

Three towers follow the scheme correctly. In these towers, every hat is placed according to the required rule.

![explainA]  ![explainB]  ![explainC]


D. One tower breaks the rule at one of its levels. This tower therefore does not follow the scheme and is the correct answer.

![explainD]

Another way to analyze the solution is to realize that the bidirectional arrows allow either of the two hats next to them to be above or below. Therefore, you only need to consider the order of the hats where there are arrows in only one direction.

The only restriction, therefore, is that the blue hat with circles cannot be on top of the orange hat or the light blue hat. In fact, it cannot be on top of any of the other hats in the chart.

## This is Informatics

In informatics, rules or instructions are often used to describe how objects should be arranged or processed.

Such rules can be written as algorithms or schemes that specify how elements should be organized. By following these rules step by step, a computer program can generate structures or verify whether a structure is correct.

In this task, the scheme represents a set of rules describing how hats may be stacked in a tower. The task is to check which tower follows these rules and which one does not.
The scheme can also be interpreted as a simple automaton, a concept widely used in computer science. In an automaton, there are a number of states and the number transitions between states that are represented by arrows. Here, each type of hat corresponds to a state, and the arrows indicate which hat can be placed on top of another.

Some transitions can be repeated many times, forming loops. This means that certain hats can appear repeatedly in a tower as long as the stacking follows the allowed transitions.

Automaton and similar rule-based models are used in computer science to verify structures, recognize patterns, process sequences, and ensure that systems follow specified rules.

## This is Computational Thinking

To solve this task, participants must carefully analyze the pattern described by the scheme.
First, they need to interpret the rules shown in the scheme and understand which hats may be placed on top of others. This requires abstraction, because the visual scheme represents rules that must be applied to every tower.

Next, participants examine each tower step by step. For every pair of neighboring hats, they check whether the transition follows the rule shown in the scheme. This process requires algorithmic thinking, because the same checking procedure must be applied repeatedly.

Participants must also use pattern recognition to notice which hat combinations are allowed and which are not.

Finally, they perform systematic checking and logical reasoning to determine which tower breaks the rule.
These skills—interpreting rules, recognizing patterns, and applying a step-by-step procedure—are important elements of computational thinking, which helps people analyze and design processes that computers can execute.
